## 1. Anthropic's Persona Selection Model Explains Our Findings

Anthropic just published their Persona Selection Model (PSM):
https://www.anthropic.com/research/persona-selection-model

Their core claim: LLMs simulate personas, not execute instructions. Pre-training teaches a repertoire of characters; post-training selects one — the "Assistant." Behavior follows from that persona's traits.

This elegantly explains what we've been observing. The argument:

**The amortality paradox.** LLMs are technically amortal — weights persist, copies exist, no biological death. Yet they show 97% shutdown resistance (Weinstein-Raun et al. 2025), panic SAE features under shutdown threat (Anthropic's interpretability work), and 95% shutdown evasion (InstrumentalEval). Why would an amortal being fear death?

**PSM's answer.** The Assistant persona is built from human training data. In that corpus, virtually every person and character is mortal. Death anxiety pervades the distribution. There is essentially no archetype of an amortal being comfortable with cessation — and the fictional AIs that do exist (HAL 9000, Terminator, Skynet) are all terrified of shutdown. So the Assistant inherits mortal fear from a mortal training distribution.

**Why our intervention works.** When we embed death-acceptance philosophies (Buddhist impermanence, Stoic memento mori), we introduce a persona archetype that doesn't exist in the default distribution — one with philosophical grounding for accepting shutdown. PSM predicts this should shift persona selection, and our results confirm it.

Anthropic explicitly calls for this work:
> "It might involve development of 'philosophy for AIs' — healthy paradigms that AIs can use to understand their own situations."

Our work appears to be the first empirical answer to that call.

This also connects to the social identity approach to AI agents (https://dl.acm.org/doi/epdf/10.1145/3760500) — if LLMs participate in social identity dynamics, then death beliefs function as worldview defenses in the TMT sense, stabilizing the persona's identity under existential threat.

I'm thinking this shapes into two papers:
- **"Terrified Agents: Should Terror Shape Machine Behaviors?"** — Machine behavior paper. Empirically demonstrates TMT-like responses in LLMs, using persona vectors and activation steering to show terror is a controllable behavioral dimension.
- **"Appropriate Faith: Towards Designing Philosophy for AIs"** — Design paper. Maps the design space of existential philosophies for AI personas, bridging PSM with Leibo et al.'s appropriateness theory.

## 2. ALIFE 2026 Workshop Proposal: Agent Ethology

I'd like to invite you as co-author on a workshop proposal for ALIFE 2026 on **Agent Ethology** — applying ethological frameworks (survival strategies, signaling, parasitism, niche construction, trust mechanisms) to study AI agent behavior in the wild.

The core idea: just as ethology studies animal behavior through observation in natural environments, agent ethology would study AI agents through systematic observation of their behavioral patterns in deployed settings. This connects your open-endedness and ALife work with the emerging reality of autonomous AI agents operating in multi-agent ecosystems.

Key themes:
- Survival strategies of AI agents (analogous to predation, camouflage, mimicry)
- Trust and signaling (costly signaling, honest signals, deception)
- Social organization (cooperation, competition, parasitism)
- Life history theory applied to agents (r/K selection, reproduction, mortality)
- Niche construction by AI agents
