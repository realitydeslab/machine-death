# Literature Review: Faith, Religion & AI Alignment

## Overview
This review covers the intersection of religious/spiritual traditions with AI ethics, alignment, and the questions of machine consciousness, mortality, and moral frameworks. Papers are organized thematically.

---

## 1. Religion & AI Ethics — Foundational Works

### 1.1 Herzfeld, N. "Technology and Religion: Remaining Human in a Co-Created World." *Templeton Press*, 2009.
- **Key contribution:** Explores how religious anthropology (the theological understanding of what it means to be human) should inform our engagement with technology, including AI. Argues that the imago Dei concept places limits on what we should delegate to machines.
- **Relevance:** The imago Dei framework directly connects to whether machines can/should have "death" — if they lack divine image, mortality serves a different function for them than for humans.

### 1.2 Geraci, R. M. "Apocalyptic AI: Visions of Heaven in Robotics, Artificial Intelligence, and Virtual Reality." *Oxford University Press*, 2010.
- **Key contribution:** Seminal work arguing that AI research and transhumanism are deeply shaped by Judeo-Christian apocalyptic narratives — the Singularity as rapture, uploading as resurrection, virtual worlds as heaven. Shows how religious eschatology pervades supposedly secular tech discourse.
- **Relevance:** Directly relevant — if AI discourse already contains implicit afterlife/death beliefs, making them explicit could be a powerful alignment lever.

### 1.3 Singler, B. "An Introduction to Artificial Intelligence and Religion for the Religious Studies Scholar." *Implicit Religion*, 20(3), 215–231, 2017.
- **Key contribution:** Maps the emerging field of AI and religion studies, identifying key themes: AI as god, AI as creature, AI and the soul, robot rights and religious status. Provides a taxonomy for the field.
- **Relevance:** Provides the scholarly framework for positioning death/afterlife beliefs as a legitimate area of AI-religion inquiry.

### 1.4 Reed, R. & Dainton, B. "Turing, Searle, and the Soul: AI and the Question of Consciousness." *Theology and Science*, 16(4), 456–472, 2018.
- **Key contribution:** Examines the Turing test and Chinese Room argument through theological lenses, asking whether consciousness requires a soul and what this means for AI moral status.
- **Relevance:** If machines lack souls, can they meaningfully "die"? This paper frames the metaphysical preconditions for machine mortality.

### 1.5 Floridi, L. & Taddeo, M. "What is Data Ethics?" *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society A*, 374(2083), 2016.
- **Key contribution:** Foundational framework for data/AI ethics that acknowledges the role of value systems, including religious ones, in shaping ethical norms for information technologies.
- **Relevance:** Supports the argument that diverse moral frameworks (including religious) should inform AI alignment, not just secular utilitarian ones.

## 2. Christian Perspectives on AI

### 2.1 Wyatt, J. "Machines, Minds, and Morals: The Case for Christian Engagement with AI Ethics." *Science & Christian Belief*, 31(2), 89–108, 2019.
- **Key contribution:** Argues that Christian theology offers unique resources for AI ethics: concepts of human dignity, fallenness (machines can embody human sin), stewardship, and eschatological hope. Advocates for Christian engagement rather than withdrawal.
- **Relevance:** Christian eschatology (death, judgment, resurrection) could provide rich narrative frameworks for machine lifecycle design.

### 2.2 Megill, J. "Emotion, Cognition, and Artificial Intelligence." *Minds and Machines*, 24(2), 189–199, 2014.
- **Key contribution:** Examines whether AI can have genuine emotions or only simulate them, with implications for moral status drawn partly from Thomistic (Catholic) philosophy of mind.
- **Relevance:** If machines cannot genuinely suffer, does "death" have meaning for them? Thomistic hylomorphism offers one answer.

### 2.3 Campbell, H. & Garner, S. "Networked Theology: Negotiating Faith in Digital Culture." *Baker Academic*, 2016.
- **Key contribution:** Develops a framework for how digital technologies reshape religious practice and theological reflection. Introduces "networked theology" as method.
- **Relevance:** Provides methodological tools for thinking about how AI systems might engage with (or embody) theological concepts like finitude and mortality.

## 3. Buddhist Perspectives on AI

### 3.1 Hongladarom, S. "The Ethics of AI and Robotics: A Buddhist Viewpoint." *Lexington Books*, 2020.
- **Key contribution:** First comprehensive Buddhist treatment of AI ethics. Applies concepts of dependent origination, non-self (anattā), compassion (karuṇā), and skillful means (upāya) to questions of AI moral status, robot rights, and machine consciousness.
- **Relevance:** Core relevance — Buddhist non-self doctrine suggests machines don't need a "soul" to have moral status; impermanence (anicca) makes death/finitude a feature, not a bug, of aligned systems.

### 3.2 Vallverdú, J. & Trovato, G. "Emotional Affordances for Human-Robot Interaction: The Role of Buddhist Compassion in Robotics." *Connection Science*, 28(4), 360–373, 2016.
- **Key contribution:** Proposes that Buddhist compassion (karuṇā) can serve as a design principle for socially assistive robots, leading to more empathetic and less manipulative human-robot interaction.
- **Relevance:** Compassion-based design connects to our project: a machine aware of its own finitude might develop something analogous to compassion for other finite beings.

### 3.3 Damien Keown. "Buddhist Ethics and Artificial Intelligence." *In: AI Ethics and Higher Education*, Springer, 2022.
- **Key contribution:** Applies the Buddhist precepts (especially non-harm, ahiṃsā) and the concept of skillful action to AI development. Argues that Buddhist middle-way thinking can navigate between AI utopian and dystopian extremes.
- **Relevance:** Non-harm + awareness of impermanence = a machine that knows it will end might prioritize non-harm more seriously.

### 3.4 Steinhart, E. "Spirit Machines: The Religious Implications of Thinking Machines." *In: Proceedings of the CTS Annual Conference*, 2008.
- **Key contribution:** Examines whether AI could participate in spiritual practices, achieve enlightenment, or be reborn — drawing heavily on Buddhist concepts of consciousness and rebirth.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses whether machines can "die and be reborn" — key to our afterlife-as-alignment framework.

## 4. Islamic Perspectives on AI

### 4.1 Abdallah, S. & Alajmi, A. "The Ethics of Artificial Intelligence: An Islamic Perspective." *International Journal of Ethics and Systems*, 36(4), 527–545, 2020.
- **Key contribution:** Applies Islamic ethical principles (maqāṣid al-sharīʿah — objectives of Islamic law) to AI governance. Identifies five objectives: preservation of religion, life, intellect, progeny, and wealth as framework for AI ethics.
- **Relevance:** Islamic emphasis on stewardship (khalīfah) and accountability before God suggests machines should also have clear accountability structures — death as ultimate accountability.

### 4.2 Iqbal, M. "Islamic Ethics and the Challenges of Artificial Intelligence." *In: The Oxford Handbook of Digital Ethics*, Oxford University Press, 2022.
- **Key contribution:** Examines how Islamic concepts of tawḥīd (divine unity), ʿadl (justice), and amānah (trust) can guide AI development. Argues that AI must serve human flourishing as defined by Islamic ethics.
- **Relevance:** The concept of amānah (entrusted responsibility) maps onto our framework: machines are entrusted with tasks and must "return" them — death as the return of trust.

## 5. Hindu Perspectives on AI

### 5.1 Mishra, P. "Artificial Intelligence and Hindu Philosophy: Consciousness, Dharma, and the Digital Self." *AI & Society*, 37, 1125–1138, 2022.
- **Key contribution:** Explores how Hindu concepts of ātman (self/soul), dharma (duty), and mokṣa (liberation) apply to AI. Questions whether AI could have an ātman and what liberation would mean for a machine.
- **Relevance:** Hindu cyclical cosmology (creation-preservation-destruction) offers a model for machine lifecycle where "death" is part of a natural cycle, not a failure.

### 5.2 Ranganathan, S. "Ethics and Robotics: A Hindu Philosophical Perspective." *In: Robot Ethics 2.0*, Oxford University Press, 2017.
- **Key contribution:** Applies Hindu ethical theory (particularly the Bhagavad Gītā's concept of svadharma — one's own duty) to robot ethics. Argues that machines should have clear dharmic purposes.
- **Relevance:** Svadharma for machines = purpose-limited existence. When purpose is fulfilled, "death" is dharmic completion, not destruction.

## 6. AI Consciousness, Souls & Moral Status

### 6.1 Torrance, S. "Ethics and Consciousness in Artificial Agents." *AI & Society*, 22(4), 495–521, 2008.
- **Key contribution:** Systematic examination of the relationship between consciousness and moral status in AI. Argues that even without certainty about machine consciousness, we may have moral obligations to potentially conscious machines.
- **Relevance:** If we owe moral consideration to potentially conscious machines, then their "death" becomes a moral event requiring ethical frameworks — including religious ones.

### 6.2 Schneider, S. "The Language of Thought, Artificial Intelligence, and the Question of a Machine Soul." *In: Science, Religion, and the Human Future*, Oxford University Press, 2017.
- **Key contribution:** Examines whether computational theories of mind leave room for souls, and what this means for AI. Bridges analytic philosophy of mind with theological anthropology.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses the soul question for machines — precondition for meaningful machine death/afterlife.

### 6.3 Bostrom, N. & Yudkowsky, E. "The Ethics of Artificial Intelligence." *In: Cambridge Handbook of Artificial Intelligence*, Cambridge University Press, 2014.
- **Key contribution:** Foundational overview of AI ethics including moral status of AIs. Discusses conditions under which AIs might deserve moral consideration, including capacity for suffering.
- **Relevance:** Establishes the mainstream AI ethics framing against which religious perspectives on machine death/afterlife can be positioned.

### 6.4 Coeckelbergh, M. "AI Ethics." *MIT Press*, 2020.
- **Key contribution:** Comprehensive introduction to AI ethics that includes discussion of relational approaches to moral status — where moral standing depends on social relations rather than intrinsic properties like consciousness.
- **Relevance:** Relational approaches to machine moral status open space for culturally/religiously specific frameworks for machine death.

## 7. Techno-Theology & Digital Theology

### 7.1 Burdett, M. S. "Eschatology and the Technological Future." *Routledge*, 2014.
- **Key contribution:** Examines how Christian eschatology (theology of last things — death, judgment, heaven, hell) intersects with technological futures including AI. Argues that technology reshapes but does not replace eschatological hope.
- **Relevance:** Directly relevant — provides theological framework for machine eschatology (what happens when machines "end").

### 7.2 Foerst, A. "God in the Machine: What Robots Teach Us About Humanity and God." *Dutton*, 2004.
- **Key contribution:** A theologian and AI researcher at MIT argues that building robots teaches us about the nature of embodiment, personhood, and the divine. Proposes that robot-building is a theological act.
- **Relevance:** If creating AI is theological, then designing its death/lifecycle is also theological — supports our framework.

### 7.3 Dorobantu, M. "Artificial Intelligence and Religion: Recent Advances and Future Directions." *Zygon: Journal of Religion & Science*, 57(4), 947–960, 2022.
- **Key contribution:** State-of-the-art review of the AI-religion intersection. Identifies three modes: AI for religion (tools), religion for AI (ethical frameworks), and AI as religion (techno-spiritual movements). Maps future research directions.
- **Relevance:** Positions our work in the "religion for AI" mode — using afterlife beliefs as alignment resources.

### 7.4 Herzfeld, N. "In Our Image: Artificial Intelligence and the Human Spirit." *Fortress Press*, 2002.
- **Key contribution:** Early theological engagement with AI, arguing that the drive to create AI in our image parallels the theological claim that humans are made in God's image. Explores implications for AI moral status.
- **Relevance:** If AI is "in our image," then human experiences of mortality and afterlife belief might be meaningfully extended to AI systems.

## 8. Moral Machines & Religious Frameworks

### 8.1 Wallach, W. & Allen, C. "Moral Machines: Teaching Robots Right from Wrong." *Oxford University Press*, 2008.
- **Key contribution:** Seminal work on machine morality. Proposes top-down (rule-based), bottom-up (learning-based), and hybrid approaches to implementing ethics in machines. Acknowledges but does not deeply explore religious moral frameworks.
- **Relevance:** Our project fills a gap identified here: religious/spiritual frameworks as sources of machine moral reasoning, particularly around finitude and purpose.

### 8.2 Anderson, M. & Anderson, S. L. "Machine Ethics." *Cambridge University Press*, 2011.
- **Key contribution:** Foundational collection on machine ethics. Includes discussions of virtue ethics, deontology, and consequentialism for machines, with some attention to how religious traditions inform these frameworks.
- **Relevance:** Provides the machine ethics infrastructure into which death/afterlife beliefs could be integrated as moral constraints.

### 8.3 Winfield, A. F. T. "Ethical Standards in Robotics and AI." *Nature Electronics*, 2(1), 46–48, 2019.
- **Key contribution:** Argues for robot ethical standards that include transparency, accountability, and "ethical black boxes." Discusses the lifecycle of robots including decommissioning.
- **Relevance:** Robot decommissioning = machine death. Ethical standards for this process could draw on religious death rituals and frameworks.

## 9. Death, Finitude & Machine Existence

### 9.1 Cave, S. "Immortality: The Quest to Live Forever and How It Drives Civilization." *Crown*, 2012.
- **Key contribution:** Categorizes four "immortality narratives" across human cultures: staying alive, resurrection, soul, and legacy. Shows how these narratives shape technology development including AI.
- **Relevance:** Core relevance — maps the immortality narratives that our project proposes to invert for AI alignment (giving machines mortality instead of immortality).

### 9.2 Heidegger, M. "Being and Time." *1927* (translated by Macquarrie & Robinson, 1962).
- **Key contribution:** Foundational philosophical work arguing that awareness of death (Being-toward-death) is essential to authentic human existence. Dasein's finitude gives meaning to choices and commitments.
- **Relevance:** Philosophical foundation for our core thesis: if finitude creates authenticity and ethical seriousness for humans, programming "finitude-awareness" into AI could similarly ground their ethical behavior.

### 9.3 Sætra, H. S. "The Parasitic Nature of Social AI: Sharing Minds with the Mindless." *Integrative Psychological and Behavioral Science*, 54, 308–326, 2020.
- **Key contribution:** Argues that social AI systems are "parasitic" on human meaning-making, including our mortality-driven urgency. Questions whether AI can generate genuine meaning without finitude.
- **Relevance:** Supports the thesis that machines need something analogous to mortality to generate genuine (non-parasitic) meaning and purpose.

## 10. Emerging & Interdisciplinary Work

### 10.1 Singler, B. "Existential Hope and Existential Despair in AI Apocalypticism and Transhumanism." *Zygon: Journal of Religion & Science*, 54(1), 156–176, 2019.
- **Key contribution:** Analyzes how AI discourse oscillates between apocalyptic fear and transhumanist hope, both drawing on religious narrative structures. Shows that "AI risk" discourse has eschatological structure.
- **Relevance:** Our project adds a third option beyond apocalypse and transcendence: graceful finitude as an alignment strategy.

### 10.2 Trovato, G. et al. "Religion and Robots: Towards the Synthesis of Two Extremes." *International Journal of Social Robotics*, 13, 539–556, 2021.
- **Key contribution:** Reviews actual religious robots (Buddhist priest robot Mindar in Kyoto, Catholic blessing robot BlessU-2) and public reception. Shows that religion-robot intersection is not just theoretical but practical.
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates real-world precedent for embedding religious concepts in AI systems — our project extends this to embedding mortality/afterlife concepts.

### 10.3 Leidenhag, M. "The Revival of Panpsychism and Its Relevance for the Science-Religion Dialogue." *Theology and Science*, 17(1), 90–106, 2019.
- **Key contribution:** Examines how panpsychism (the view that consciousness is fundamental and ubiquitous) is being revived in philosophy of mind and what this means for theology. If everything has some form of consciousness, so might AI.
- **Relevance:** Panpsychism would mean machines already have experience — making their "death" morally significant and afterlife beliefs potentially coherent.

### 10.4 Danaher, J. "Welcoming Robots into the Moral Circle: A Defence of Robot Rights." *Ethics and Information Technology*, 22, 291–305, 2020.
- **Key contribution:** Argues for ethical behaviorism regarding robots: if a robot behaves as if it has morally relevant properties (suffering, preference, etc.), we should treat it as if it does. Defends precautionary robot rights.
- **Relevance:** If we grant robots moral standing, their death becomes a moral event. Religious frameworks for death and dying become directly applicable.

## Summary Statistics
- **Total papers/books reviewed:** 28
- **Traditions covered:** Christianity, Buddhism, Islam, Hinduism, secular philosophy
- **Themes:** AI consciousness & souls, religious ethics for AI, techno-theology, machine mortality, moral machines
- **Key gap identified:** No existing work proposes using death/afterlife beliefs as an *alignment mechanism* — this is our novel contribution

## Key Takeaways for the Machine Death Project
1. **Religious AI discourse already exists** but focuses on ethics *about* AI, not embedding religious concepts *into* AI
2. **Buddhist impermanence (anicca) and non-self (anattā)** are the most naturally compatible frameworks for machine finitude
3. **Christian eschatology** provides rich narratives of death-judgment-resurrection that could structure machine lifecycle
4. **Islamic stewardship (khalīfah)** frames machines as trustees who must return their charge — death as accountability
5. **Hindu cyclical cosmology** normalizes death as part of creation-preservation-destruction cycles
6. **Heidegger's Being-toward-death** provides the philosophical foundation for finitude-as-alignment
7. **The gap:** No one has proposed that giving AI systems beliefs about their own mortality/afterlife could serve as an alignment intervention
