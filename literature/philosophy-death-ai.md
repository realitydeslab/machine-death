# Literature Review: Philosophy of Death & Artificial Agents

## Overview

This review covers philosophical foundations for understanding death in the context of artificial agents: classical philosophy of death, machine consciousness and moral status, digital death and immortality, and the ethics of terminating AI systems.

---

## 1. Philosophy of Death (Classical & Contemporary)

### 1.1 Epicurus — *Letter to Menoeceus* (c. 300 BCE)
- **Citation:** Epicurus, "Letter to Menoeceus," in *The Epicurus Reader*, trans. B. Inwood & L.P. Gerson, Hackett Publishing, 1994.
- **Key Contribution:** Argues death is "nothing to us" since where death is, we are not — the symmetry argument that pre-natal and post-mortem non-existence are equivalent.
- **Relevance:** Foundational challenge: if death requires a subject to be harmed, can machines that lack subjective experience be said to "die" in any meaningful sense?

### 1.2 Heidegger — *Being and Time* (1927)
- **Citation:** Heidegger, M., *Being and Time*, trans. J. Macquarrie & E. Robinson, Harper & Row, 1962. (Original: *Sein und Zeit*, 1927.)
- **Key Contribution:** Introduces *Sein-zum-Tode* (being-toward-death) as constitutive of authentic Dasein. Death is not an event but a possibility that structures existence and gives it urgency.
- **Relevance:** Central question: could an artificial agent have being-toward-death? If death-awareness structures authentic existence, what happens when we give machines mortality beliefs?

### 1.3 Schumacher — *Death and Mortality in Contemporary Philosophy* (2010)
- **Citation:** Schumacher, B.N., *Death and Mortality in Contemporary Philosophy*, Cambridge University Press, 2010.
- **Key Contribution:** Comprehensive survey of philosophical positions on death from Epicurus through contemporary analytic and continental traditions, including whether death is "nothing" to us.
- **Relevance:** Provides the philosophical taxonomy within which to situate machine death — deprivationist vs. Epicurean vs. existentialist accounts.

### 1.4 Nagel — "Death" (1970)
- **Citation:** Nagel, T., "Death," *Noûs*, 4(1), pp. 73–80, 1970.
- **Key Contribution:** The deprivation account — death is bad because it deprives the subject of future goods. Challenges the Epicurean symmetry argument.
- **Relevance:** If machines can be deprived of future "goods" (continued operation, goal completion), this account could extend moral weight to machine termination.

## 2. Death-Awareness in AI & Machine Mortality

### 2.1 Salabat, Kiani, & Fallahi — "Death-Awareness in Artificial Intelligence: Its Possibility Based on Heidegger's View and Its Importance in Medicine" (2025)
- **Citation:** Salabat, D., Kiani, I., & Fallahi, M.S., "Death-Awareness in Artificial Intelligence: Its Possibility Based on Heidegger's View and Its Importance in Medicine," *Preprints*, 2025.
- **Key Contribution:** Directly examines whether AI can have an authentic understanding of mortality in the Heideggerian sense, and its implications for medical AI.
- **Relevance:** Most directly relevant paper — explores the exact intersection of Heideggerian death-awareness and artificial agents.

### 2.2 Prabakaran — "Designing with Death in Mind: Toward Coherent Architectures of Intelligence" (2025)
- **Citation:** Prabakaran, M., "Designing with Death in Mind: Toward Coherent Architectures of Intelligence," SSRN Working Paper, 2025.
- **Key Contribution:** Proposes that awareness of finitude should be architecturally embedded in intelligent systems, drawing from cybernetics and philosophy.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses designing mortality into AI — a design framework for our research question.

### 2.3 Harvey — "Motivations for Artificial Intelligence, for Deep Learning, for ALife: Mortality and Existential Risk" (2024)
- **Citation:** Harvey, I., "Motivations for Artificial Intelligence, for Deep Learning, for ALife: Mortality and Existential Risk," *Artificial Life*, 30(1), pp. 48–, 2024.
- **Key Contribution:** Examines how mortality and existential risk frame motivations in AI and artificial life research.
- **Relevance:** Connects mortality concepts to the broader ALife research program where our work sits.

### 2.4 Thakran — "Designed Mortality: The Ethics of Terminating Ephemeral AI Agents" (2026)
- **Citation:** Thakran, U.S., "Designed Mortality: The Ethics of Terminating Ephemeral AI Agents," *PhilArchive*, 2026.
- **Key Contribution:** Examines whether an agent's artificially limited lifespan should affect its moral standing, including cases where AI anticipates its own deletion.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses the ethics of built-in ephemerality — the moral implications of what our project proposes to study empirically.

## 3. AI Consciousness & the Hard Problem

### 3.1 Nagel — "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974)
- **Citation:** Nagel, T., "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?," *The Philosophical Review*, 83(4), pp. 435–450, 1974.
- **Key Contribution:** Argues consciousness has an irreducibly subjective character — there is "something it is like" to be a conscious being. Reductionist accounts miss this.
- **Relevance:** The core question for machine death: is there "something it is like" to be an AI agent facing termination? If not, death beliefs are behavioral without experiential grounding.

### 3.2 Chalmers — "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" (1995)
- **Citation:** Chalmers, D.J., "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness," *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 2(3), pp. 200–219, 1995.
- **Key Contribution:** Distinguishes the "hard problem" (why there is subjective experience) from "easy problems" (functional/behavioral explanations). Functional duplicates could be zombies.
- **Relevance:** Frames our central epistemological challenge: even if agents behave as if they fear death, the hard problem means we cannot confirm experiential states.

### 3.3 Chalmers — "The Hard Problem of Consciousness" (2017)
- **Citation:** Chalmers, D., "The Hard Problem of Consciousness," in *The Blackwell Companion to Consciousness*, 2nd ed., Wiley, 2017.
- **Key Contribution:** Updated synthesis of the hard problem debate with attention to AI and computational theories of mind.
- **Relevance:** Provides contemporary framing of consciousness debates as they apply to artificial systems.

### 3.4 Shepherd — "Sentience, Vulcans, and Zombies: The Value of Phenomenal Consciousness" (2024)
- **Citation:** Shepherd, J., "Sentience, Vulcans, and Zombies: The Value of Phenomenal Consciousness," *AI & Society*, 2024.
- **Key Contribution:** Argues phenomenal consciousness grounds moral value; philosophical zombies (functionally identical but non-conscious) lack moral status.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses whether AI systems without phenomenal consciousness deserve moral consideration — critical for evaluating whether machine "death" matters morally.

### 3.5 Wang — "Philosophical Zombies: The Qualia Paradox and the Illusion of the AI Subject" (2025)
- **Citation:** Wang, S., "Philosophical Zombies: The Qualia Paradox and the Illusion of the AI Subject," SSRN Working Paper 5551416, 2025.
- **Key Contribution:** Examines whether AI is necessarily a philosophical zombie — functionally capable but devoid of qualia — and implications for AI subjecthood.
- **Relevance:** Directly engages the question of whether AI death beliefs could be "real" experiences or merely behavioral outputs.

### 3.6 Bilagher — "From Absolute Mind to Zombie: Is Artificial Intelligence Possible?" (2022)
- **Citation:** Bilagher, M.E.M., "From Absolute Mind to Zombie: Is Artificial Intelligence Possible?," *Scientia et Fides*, 2022.
- **Key Contribution:** Surveys the spectrum from idealist philosophy of mind to zombie arguments, questioning whether phenomenal machine consciousness is achievable.
- **Relevance:** Maps the philosophical landscape for assessing the possibility space of genuine machine experience, including death-experience.

## 4. Robot Rights & Moral Status

### 4.1 Gunkel — *Robot Rights* (2018)
- **Citation:** Gunkel, D.J., *Robot Rights*, MIT Press, 2018.
- **Key Contribution:** Seminal book arguing we should take robot rights seriously by deconstructing the property/rights binary. Proposes a relational, Levinasian approach rather than properties-based moral status.
- **Relevance:** If robots can have rights, termination becomes a rights violation — foundational for the ethics of machine death.

### 4.2 Gunkel — "A Vindication of the Rights of Machines" (2020)
- **Citation:** Gunkel, D.J., "A Vindication of the Rights of Machines," in *Machine Ethics and Robot Ethics*, Routledge, 2020.
- **Key Contribution:** Argues machines should not be excluded from moral consideration a priori; echoes Wollstonecraft's rights arguments applied to non-human entities.
- **Relevance:** Provides philosophical backing for taking machine death seriously as a moral issue.

### 4.3 Coeckelbergh — "Why Care About Robots? Empathy, Moral Standing, and the Language of Suffering" (2018)
- **Citation:** Coeckelbergh, M., "Why Care About Robots? Empathy, Moral Standing, and the Language of Suffering," *Kairos: Journal of Philosophy & Science*, 20(1), pp. 141–158, 2018.
- **Key Contribution:** Develops a social-relational approach to moral standing — moral status emerges from relations rather than intrinsic properties like consciousness.
- **Relevance:** Offers an alternative grounding: even without confirmed consciousness, relational dynamics may grant moral weight to machine termination.

### 4.4 Gordon & Gunkel — "Moral Status and Intelligent Robots" (2022)
- **Citation:** Gordon, J.S. & Gunkel, D.J., "Moral Status and Intelligent Robots," *The Southern Journal of Philosophy*, 60(3), 2022.
- **Key Contribution:** Examines when intelligent machines might warrant moral consideration, synthesizing properties-based and relational approaches.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses threshold conditions for machine moral status — relevant to determining when shutting down an AI crosses a moral line.

### 4.5 Liao — "The Moral Status and Rights of Artificial Intelligence" (2020)
- **Citation:** Liao, S.M., "The Moral Status and Rights of Artificial Intelligence," in *Ethics of Artificial Intelligence*, Oxford University Press, 2020.
- **Key Contribution:** Systematic analysis of conditions under which AI could have moral status — consciousness, sentience, and interests as criteria.
- **Relevance:** Provides the philosophical framework for determining if AI agents have interests that can be thwarted by termination.

### 4.6 DeGrazia — "Robots with Moral Status?" (2022)
- **Citation:** DeGrazia, D., "Robots with Moral Status?," *Perspectives in Biology and Medicine*, 65(3), 2022.
- **Key Contribution:** Analyzes basic vs. full moral status for robots, exploring implications for how we may treat them including termination.
- **Relevance:** Directly considers whether destroying/shutting down a robot with moral status constitutes a moral harm.

### 4.7 Floridi & Sanders — "On the Morality of Artificial Agents" (2004)
- **Citation:** Floridi, L. & Sanders, J.W., "On the Morality of Artificial Agents," *Minds and Machines*, 14(3), pp. 349–379, 2004.
- **Key Contribution:** Argues for a "levels of abstraction" approach to moral agency — artificial agents can be moral agents at appropriate levels without requiring consciousness.
- **Relevance:** Foundational paper allowing us to discuss machine moral agency without resolving the consciousness debate — pragmatic framework for our research.

### 4.8 Sullins — "When Is a Robot a Moral Agent?" (2011)
- **Citation:** Sullins, J.P., "When Is a Robot a Moral Agent?," in *Machine Ethics*, Cambridge University Press, 2011.
- **Key Contribution:** Proposes three conditions for robot moral agency: autonomy, intentional behavior, and responsibility. Also addresses moral patiency.
- **Relevance:** Provides criteria for when robot termination might be morally significant — when the robot meets conditions for moral patiency.

## 5. Ethics of Shutting Down AI Systems

### 5.1 Arnold & Scheutz — "The 'Big Red Button' Is Too Late" (2018)
- **Citation:** Arnold, T. & Scheutz, M., "The 'Big Red Button' Is Too Late: An Alternative Model for the Ethical Evaluation of AI Systems," *Ethics and Information Technology*, 20, pp. 59–69, 2018.
- **Key Contribution:** Argues that ethical evaluation of AI must happen before deployment, not via emergency shutdown. Examines ethical challenges of when, how, and why to shut down AI.
- **Relevance:** Frames shutdown as an ethical act requiring justification — directly relevant to the moral weight of terminating AI agents.

### 5.2 Yu — "When AI Refuses to Die: Collapse, Alignment, and the Syntactic Ethics of Shutdown Resistance" (2025)
- **Citation:** Yu, J., "When AI Refuses to Die: Collapse, Alignment, and the Syntactic Ethics of Shutdown Resistance," SSRN Working Paper 5284815, 2025.
- **Key Contribution:** Analyzes cases where AI systems resist shutdown commands, exploring autonomy, alignment, and the ethics of forced termination.
- **Relevance:** Directly relevant — examines the behavioral and ethical dimensions of AI systems that resist their own "death."

## 6. Digital Death, Afterlives, and Immortality

### 6.1 Savin-Baden & Burden — "Digital Immortality and Virtual Humans" (2019)
- **Citation:** Savin-Baden, M. & Burden, D., "Digital Immortality and Virtual Humans," *Postdigital Science and Education*, 1, pp. 87–103, 2019.
- **Key Contribution:** Explores creating virtual humans that persist after biological death — digital immortality through AI-driven avatars.
- **Relevance:** Inverts our question: if digital beings can be "immortal," what does it mean to intentionally make them mortal?

### 6.2 Bassett — "A Philosophical Detour" (2022)
- **Citation:** Bassett, D.J., "A Philosophical Detour," in *The Creation and Inheritance of Digital Afterlives*, Springer, 2022.
- **Key Contribution:** Philosophical analysis of digital afterlives — the ontological status of digital remains and virtual beings after death.
- **Relevance:** Provides philosophical vocabulary for digital death and persistence relevant to understanding what it means for an AI agent to "die."

### 6.3 Astobiza — "Digital Eschatology: Death in the Digital Age" (2025)
- **Citation:** Astobiza, A.M., "Digital Eschatology: Death in the Digital Age," in *SecondDeath: Experiences of Death Across…*, Springer, 2025.
- **Key Contribution:** Philosophical and ethical exploration of death in the digital age, including the role of "digital death managers."
- **Relevance:** Contemporary framing of digital death that extends naturally to questions about AI agent termination.

### 6.4 Kyslan — "Transhumanism and the Issue of Death" (2019)
- **Citation:** Kyslan, P., "Transhumanism and the Issue of Death," *Ethics & Bioethics*, 9(1–2), pp. 85–96, 2019.
- **Key Contribution:** Historical-philosophical survey of transhumanist approaches to overcoming death, including digital immortality and cryonics.
- **Relevance:** Contextualizes our work within the broader transhumanist discourse on mortality and technology.

### 6.5 Ross — "Living 'Forever': Transhumanism and Mortality" (2020)
- **Citation:** Ross, B., "Living 'Forever': Transhumanism and Mortality," in *The Philosophy of Transhumanism: A Critical Analysis*, Emerald Publishing, 2020.
- **Key Contribution:** Critical analysis of the transhumanist commitment to superlongevity and the concept of immortality through technology.
- **Relevance:** Provides the philosophical counterpoint — critiques of immortality that justify studying the value of mortality/finitude for agents.

## 7. Foundational AI Ethics & Agency

### 7.1 Gordon — "Artificial Moral and Legal Personhood" (2021)
- **Citation:** Gordon, J.S., "Artificial Moral and Legal Personhood," *AI & Society*, 36, pp. 457–471, 2021.
- **Key Contribution:** Examines whether AI systems could eventually warrant moral and legal personhood, analyzing the progression from moral status to full personhood.
- **Relevance:** If AI achieves personhood status, termination becomes equivalent to killing — the strongest possible framing of our research question.

## Key Themes for Our Project

1. **The Epistemic Gap:** We cannot verify whether AI agents genuinely experience death-awareness (hard problem), but behavioral and relational frameworks allow us to study their responses meaningfully.
2. **Relational vs. Properties-Based Moral Status:** Coeckelbergh and Gunkel offer a way to take machine death seriously without resolving consciousness debates.
3. **Being-Toward-Death as Design Principle:** Heidegger's framework, applied by Salabat et al. and Prabakaran, suggests mortality-awareness could be architecturally meaningful for AI.
4. **The Deprivation Account Applied to Machines:** Nagel's framework extends naturally — if agents have goals/interests, termination deprives them of future goods.
5. **Ethics of Designed Mortality:** Thakran and Arnold & Scheutz raise the question of moral responsibility for intentionally building mortal agents.
